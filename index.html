01 · The moat

What makes ORVÉA hard to copy

Before any decision about software, one question matters most: what makes ORVÉA defensible? Not the brand, not the app. The answer is the household record, and understanding it explains every technology choice that follows.

The household record is the complete, structured memory of each home and how its owner likes it run. Not notes in someone's head or scattered across emails, but organised knowledge: how to get in, the alarm codes, the layout, the brands to stock, the allergies, which cleaner already knows the house, what has been fixed and when, and what "ready" looks like for this specific home.

A competitor with more money can copy our app in a month. They cannot copy years of accumulated knowledge about specific families and their homes. The record compounds: every visit, every preference learned, every job done makes it deeper and makes ORVÉA harder to replace. That is a moat in the real sense. It grows with time, and leaving means losing it.

Before

Run from memory

A home run from memory and scattered messages. Every arrival is a fresh effort. Knowledge lives with whoever happened to handle it last.

After

Run from the record

A home run from a record that already knows the family. The house is right before they walk in, without being asked, because the knowledge is held and acted on.

Before you ask.

The member never experiences the gap between wanting something and having it, because the record closed it first.

03 · Why this approach

Being wrong should be cheap

We validate the things most likely to be wrong before spending on the things that are easy to change.

We validate the things most likely to be wrong (the record, the process, the quality standard) before spending on the things that are easy to change (screens and automation).
The household record is treated as a first-class asset from the first home. Standard off-the-shelf tools scatter this knowledge across a CRM, a task list and a notes app, and never make it the asset it should be.
A structured record migrates cleanly to a larger system later. Reassembling that knowledge from three disconnected tools is a slow, lossy migration we would rather never face.
Tens of dollars a month to prove the model, against a broad platform costing roughly $11,000 to $24,000 to set up and $500 to $1,300 a month before a single client is validated.

04 · The experience

The honest answer: deferring it is a sequencing choice, not a sacrifice. A screen is a window onto the record and the process. Building it before those are proven means polishing something about to change. And because the data is structured and open, a real client experience becomes cheap to add whenever we want it.

06 · Security, honestly

Discretion is the product

So security deserves a straight answer at each stage.

Now

Secure enough to prove the model

  • Client login
  • Each client sees only their own information
  • Encrypted storage

Low effort to set up on the early tooling, and appropriate for a pilot with trusted early users.

Later · Stage 2

Bank-grade for the most security-conscious client

  • Full data residency control
  • Masking which home is empty
  • Complete audit trails
  • Isolated environments

Not fully solvable on the early tooling, under any approach we might choose.

A Stage 2 conversation, by design.

The MVP is built to prove the model with people who trust us, not to onboard the most security-conscious client on day one.
